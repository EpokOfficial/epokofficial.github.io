THE ORIGIN OF PANTHERS & VAMPIRES: BLUE MOON

I started writing Panthers & Vampires: Blue Moon after losing my birth mother. That kind of loss changes your timeline. Writing became a place to put grief when normal life didn’t have space for it. But after that, I hit a freeze that lasted years—like my mind wanted to speak, but the words wouldn’t move. For about three years, the story sat inside me, unfinished, waiting on the version of me who could carry it.

When I first broke through that freeze, the original five chapters weren’t supernatural yet. It started as an urban street love fairytale—real emotion, real pain, real people—because that’s what my life looked like. Every character in this world is rooted in personality: family, friends, and people I’ve been close to in real life. It’s fiction, but it’s not fake. It’s my way of translating real relationships, real loyalty, and real pressure into a story that can live bigger than one moment.

Then my brother passed, and I found myself drowning in a depression and in a unhealthy, toxic relationship with a very hateful woman—and it put urgency on everything. It pushed me to finish what I kept putting off, because tomorrow isn’t promised and legacy doesn’t wait. About three months ago, I had an epiphany. Not only did I snap back into who I’ve always been, but I also gained a clear vision for the book and for what truly interests me. The stories I’ve always been drawn to are vampires, witches, shapeshifters, and the supernatural.

So I went back and rewrote the foundation. I rebuilt the first five chapters, created the prologue, and shifted the story into the world it was always trying to become. That meant research. That meant Salem. That meant history. The book pulls from the weight of the Salem Witch Trials era and the realities surrounding it, and it also confronts the bloodline trauma tied to the slavery of Black people and the systems that shaped America’s earliest horror stories.

Inside the story, that same collision is everywhere—modern chaos and ancient forces sharing the same streets. You see it in the hospital aftermath, in what’s at stake, and in how fast the world turns when destiny gets activated.

You also see it in the past—like 1692—where survival, power, and lineage are treated like crimes, and where the supernatural isn’t fantasy. It’s a language people used to endure what they couldn’t escape.

At its core, this book is about people stepping into who they really are—stepping into destiny, stepping into position, stepping into their EPòK. Blue Moon is only the beginning. This is a series with multiple books already outlined, and this first release is the foundation for everything that’s coming next.
